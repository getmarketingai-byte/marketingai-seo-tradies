Marketing for Tradies and Construction Businesses — What Actually Works

Practical strategies for plumbers, electricians, builders, and other trades who want consistent work — without relying on word of mouth alone.

If you run a trade or construction business — whether you're a plumber, electrician, builder, carpenter, landscaper, or any other trade — you've probably built most of your client base through word of mouth, recommendations, and being on a builder's preferred list.

That works when times are good. But relying on it exclusively means your workload fluctuates with whoever happened to mention you last month.

The tradies who maintain consistent work without feast-and-famine cycles have typically added one or two systematic channels alongside word of mouth. This guide covers what those channels are, and how to implement them without spending hours on marketing you'd rather be on tools.

Why Word of Mouth Alone Eventually Lets You Down

Word of mouth is the best lead source for most tradies. The leads are warm, the trust is already there, and conversion is high. The problem is control — you can't turn it up when you're quiet or turn it down when you're slammed.

A slow January that catches you off guard isn't a random event. It's usually the result of not having a parallel channel that produces enquiries on a predictable schedule.

The goal isn't to replace word of mouth. It's to supplement it with a channel or two that you control.

The Channels That Work for Trade Businesses

1. Google Business Profile — Your Most Important Asset

For most tradies, Google Business Profile (formerly Google My Business) is the single highest-leverage marketing tool available — and it's free.

When someone searches "plumber [suburb]" or "emergency electrician [city]" on their phone, the Google Map pack (the three businesses that appear with ratings and a map) gets the majority of clicks. A complete, well-reviewed profile puts you there.

What to do:

  • Claim and complete your profile. Business name, service area, hours, services offered, photos of your work (before and after where relevant), and a direct booking or contact link.
  • Get reviews consistently. A business with 80 reviews at 4.7 stars wins over one with 12 reviews at 5.0. Ask every happy client for a review — most will do it if you send them the link directly. A simple follow-up text or email after the job is done works.
  • Respond to every review. Positive and negative. Responding to negative reviews professionally is one of the most visible trust signals for prospective clients.
  • Post updates monthly. A completed job photo with a brief description of the work helps you rank for specific search terms and shows active presence.

2. Your Website — Credibility When They Google You After a Referral

Even if a prospect hears about you through a referral, the first thing they do is Google your name. If you have no website, or one that looks like it was built in 2009, that warm referral cools off fast.

A tradie website doesn't need to be complex. It needs to be:

  • Specific about what you do and where you do it. "Plumbing services in [your suburb]" is better than "plumbing services across [your region]."
  • Full of photos of your actual work. Before and after, completed jobs, your team on site. Real photos beat stock images every time.
  • Easy to contact. A phone number at the top of every page, a quote request form, and ideally a booking link.
  • Review-forward. Embed your Google reviews directly on the site.

3. Repeat Client Nurture — The Revenue Stream You're Leaving Behind

Most trades with repeat-eligible work (HVAC servicing, electrical inspections, plumbing maintenance, landscaping) dramatically underutilise their existing client base.

A client who used you once and was happy is your highest-probability next job. But if you don't contact them, they forget you exist by the time the next service is due.

A simple system:

  • Service reminder emails or SMS. "Your hot water system is due for a check — we're booking [month], want us to schedule you?" Sent automatically 11 months after a service job.
  • Seasonal outreach. "Winter's coming — now's a good time to check your heating system before the cold hits." Timely, relevant, low pressure.
  • Annual check-in. A brief touchpoint to past clients once per year maintains the relationship and regularly produces repeat and referral work.

This doesn't need to be elaborate. A short, genuine message that sounds like a person wrote it outperforms any marketing email template.

One compliance note: Email regulations in your jurisdiction typically require consent before sending marketing emails. For past clients, ensure you have a record of opt-in or that the communication qualifies as a transactional service reminder under applicable rules.

4. Before-and-After Content — Social Proof That Does the Selling

Tradies have a natural content advantage: visible, tangible results. A bathroom renovation, a new deck, a rewired switchboard, a landscaped backyard — these are content that prospects actually want to see.

Where to use it:

  • Google Business Profile (increases profile engagement and ranking)
  • Facebook (local community groups are active for trades — "anyone got a good plumber in [area]?" posts are common, and a visible Facebook presence means you get recommended)
  • Instagram (particularly for visual trades — builders, landscapers, kitchen renovators)

The approach: photograph the job, write two sentences about what was done and why, post it. That's it. Twice a week is enough. You don't need captions that sound like a marketer wrote them.

5. Local Facebook Groups — Where Your Clients Are Asking

Most local areas have active Facebook community groups where residents ask for tradie recommendations. Being active in these groups — not selling, but occasionally answering questions and having a visible profile — means you get tagged when someone asks.

This doesn't mean spamming groups. It means:

  • Being a member of the groups where your clients are
  • Having a complete Facebook profile with work photos and reviews
  • Answering genuine questions when you know the answer
  • Occasionally posting a completed local job (where the group allows it)

The tradie who gets recommended most often in these groups is usually the one who has a visible profile and has been helpful in the group before.

Putting It Together Without Killing Your After-Work Hours

The tradies who do marketing well aren't spending evenings writing blog posts. They've built simple habits:

  • Photo of every completed job before leaving site
  • Review request sent the same day the job is done
  • Service reminder set in their booking system at the time of each job
  • One post per week to Google Business Profile and Facebook

The decisions are made in advance. The execution takes 10–15 minutes per day.
